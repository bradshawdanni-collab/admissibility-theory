# Four Axioms

Admissibility Theory is grounded in four axioms. A claim must satisfy all four to be admitted as a scientific competitor.

The axioms are ordered by logical priority. Axiom 1 is primary — it is the novel contribution. Axioms 2–4 are supporting conditions that make Axiom 1 testable in practice.

---

## Axiom 1 — Discriminability *(primary)*

A claim is admissible only if there exists at least one observation `O` such that:
- the claim predicts `O`, and at least one competing explanation predicts `¬O`

or vice versa.

**Why this is stricter than Popperian falsifiability:**

Falsifiability (Popper) requires only that *some* observation could in principle refute the claim. Discriminability requires a *differential prediction* — an observation that separates the claim from its nearest competitor.

A framework that predicts the same observations as every known alternative is not a competing framework. It is a restatement. Discriminability forces the question: *what would be different if this framework were true, compared to the alternative that currently explains the same phenomena?*

This is the admission gate. A claim that passes only Popper's test but fails discriminability has not yet earned the right to compete.

---

## Axiom 2 — Specification

A claim is admissible only if its key terms are formally defined — sufficient to determine, for a given observation, whether the observation satisfies or violates the claim.

**What this requires:**

The requirement is not mathematical formalism for its own sake. It is the requirement that the definition *does work*. A term earns its place in an admissible argument by being connected to a formal object: a set, a function, a measurable quantity, a decision procedure.

A term used as analogy rather than formal object is admissible as *motivation*, not as *specification*. The distinction matters: motivation explains why a claim is plausible; specification determines whether a given observation confirms or falsifies it.

**The test:** Given a candidate observation, can a third party determine — without consulting the author — whether the observation satisfies the claim? If not, the claim is not yet specified.

---

## Axiom 3 — Boundary Conditions

A claim is admissible only if its scope is bounded — the claim states:
- the conditions under which it holds
- the conditions under which it fails or does not apply

**Why unbounded claims are weaker, not stronger:**

An unbounded claim appears maximally powerful. In practice, it is maximally uninformative. A claim that applies everywhere, under all conditions, with no stated failure modes is unfalsifiable by construction — not because it is deep, but because it has not yet specified what it is claiming.

Boundary conditions are not admissions of weakness. They are the edges that give a claim its shape.

---

## Axiom 4 — Chain of Custody

In empirical contexts, a claim is admissible only if the path from raw observation to stated conclusion is documented and checkable — such that a third party, given the same starting data, could:
- reach the same conclusion, or
- identify the precise step at which they diverge

**What this requires:**

This axiom applies equally to formal proofs (where each inference step must be checkable) and to empirical results (where data provenance, processing steps, and decision points must be traceable).

A claim unsupported by a traceable inference chain is not evidence for its conclusion. It is an assertion of it. The chain of custody makes the difference between a result that can be checked and a result that can only be trusted.

---

## Relationship Between Axioms

| Axiom | Function | Failure Mode |
|---|---|---|
| 1 — Discriminability | Separates the claim from competitors | Framework becomes a restatement |
| 2 — Specification | Makes the claim checkable | Terms function as analogy not formalism |
| 3 — Boundary Conditions | Defines the claim's scope | Claim becomes unfalsifiable by expansion |
| 4 — Chain of Custody | Connects observation to conclusion | Result becomes assertion not evidence |

All four must be satisfied. A claim that satisfies three of four is conditionally admissible pending resolution of the missing axiom — but is not yet fully admitted.
