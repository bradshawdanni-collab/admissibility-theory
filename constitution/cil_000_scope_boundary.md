# Scope Boundary — CIL-000

Admissibility Theory is a threshold condition. This document defines what lies inside and outside that threshold.

The scope boundary is constitutional. It is not a limitation imposed from outside — it is a deliberate design choice. A framework that attempts to do everything typically adjudicates nothing. CIL-000 keeps Admissibility Theory at the admission gate and prevents scope creep into the territory of domain-specific adjudication.

---

## What Admissibility Theory Does

Admissibility Theory determines whether a claim has earned the right to compete as a scientific explanation.

A claim earns that right by satisfying the Four Axioms:
1. It makes a differential prediction (Discriminability)
2. Its key terms are formally defined (Specification)
3. Its scope is bounded (Boundary Conditions)
4. Its inference chain is traceable (Chain of Custody)

Once a claim satisfies all four axioms, it is **admitted**. What happens after admission is not within the jurisdiction of Admissibility Theory.

---

## What Admissibility Theory Does Not Do

**It does not rank admitted frameworks.**

Once two competing frameworks are both admitted, the question of which is correct is left to domain-specific evaluation: empirical testing, formal proof, predictive accuracy, explanatory parsimony, or whatever criteria the relevant field applies. Admissibility Theory has no view on which admitted framework is better.

**It does not evaluate elegance, novelty, or simplicity.**

A framework may be admitted because it satisfies the four axioms while being inelegant, derivative, or complex. Aesthetic properties of frameworks are outside scope. Occam's Razor is a useful heuristic; it is not an admissibility condition.

**It does not adjudicate priority disputes.**

If two researchers independently develop equivalent admitted frameworks, the question of who deserves credit is a sociological and institutional matter, not an admissibility matter.

**It does not evaluate frameworks that are admitted but untestable.**

A framework may satisfy all four axioms — including specifying a discriminating observation — while making predictions in domains where no measurement instrument currently exists. Such frameworks are admitted (because they satisfy the axioms) but are in a state of *deferred evaluation*. Admissibility Theory records this state; it does not resolve it.

**It does not determine truth.**

Admissibility is not truth. A claim can be fully admitted and false. A claim can fail admission and happen to be correct. Admissibility is a gate to the evaluation process, not a judgment about the outcome.

---

## Why the Boundary Exists

The most common failure mode in meta-scientific frameworks is jurisdictional expansion. A framework designed to evaluate evidence quality begins evaluating evidence. A framework designed to assess logical consistency begins determining conclusions.

CIL-000 is a structural constraint that prevents Admissibility Theory from expanding into the territory it is designed to serve. The framework's authority comes from its restraint: it determines whether a claim is entitled to compete, and nothing more.

> *The program stops at the admission gate. What happens inside is not its business.*

---

## Practical Implication

When applying Admissibility Theory to a specific claim, the output is always one of:

| Output | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Admitted** | Satisfies all four axioms. Entitled to compete. |
| **Conditionally admitted** | Satisfies some axioms; specific deficiencies identified. |
| **Not yet admitted** | Does not yet satisfy the axioms. Specific failures identified. |
| **Admitted, deferred** | Satisfies axioms; discriminating observation not currently measurable. |

Admissibility Theory issues no verdict on correctness. It issues a verdict on readiness.
