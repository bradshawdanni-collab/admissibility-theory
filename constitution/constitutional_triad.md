# Constitutional Triad

The Constitutional Triad consists of three principles that govern how Admissibility Theory operates in practice. The axioms define admission conditions. The triad defines the operating logic.

The three principles function as a constitutional structure: each constrains the other two. No single principle is supreme. Conflicts between them are resolved by returning to the Four Axioms.

---

## I. The Specification Principle

> *Vocabulary is not formalism. Define your objects.*

A term earns its place in an admissible argument by being connected to a formal object — a set, a function, a measurable quantity, a decision procedure. Until that connection is made, the term is doing rhetorical rather than logical work.

**The practical test:** Replace the term with its definition. If the argument still holds, the definition is doing work. If the argument collapses or becomes circular, the term was functioning as a label, not a concept.

**What this is not:** The Specification Principle does not require that every claim be expressed in mathematical notation. It requires that key terms be defined with enough precision that a third party can determine, without consulting the author, whether a given observation satisfies or violates them.

**Common failure mode:** A framework borrows the vocabulary of an established field (Riemannian geometry, Hamiltonian mechanics, topology) without inheriting the formal content of those terms. The vocabulary functions as prestige transfer, not as specification. The Specification Principle catches this.

---

## II. The Discriminability Principle

> *Coherence is not evidence. Specify what your framework rules out.*

Two coherent, internally consistent frameworks can exist simultaneously — including two frameworks that contradict each other on every substantive point. Coherence alone does not adjudicate between them.

Only differential prediction adjudicates. The discriminability principle requires every admissible framework to name the observation that would defeat it.

**The practical test:** What observation is your framework *inconsistent with*, that its nearest competitor is *consistent with*? If no such observation can be specified, the frameworks are not competing. They are incomparable — and incomparability is not a strength.

**What this is not:** The Discriminability Principle does not require that the defeating observation be currently measurable. It requires that the observation be *specifiable* — that the author can describe what it would look like if the framework were false.

**Common failure mode:** A researcher defends a framework by demonstrating that it is internally coherent and that criticism is generated by misunderstanding. Both can be true simultaneously with the framework being wrong. Coherence and comprehension are necessary conditions for a good framework; they are not sufficient conditions for a true one.

---

## III. The Chain of Custody Principle

> *A conclusion is only as strong as the traceable path from observation to claim.*

A claim unsupported by a traceable inference chain is not evidence for its conclusion. It is an assertion of it. The Chain of Custody Principle requires the inference path from raw observation to stated conclusion to be made visible and checkable.

**The practical test:** Given the same starting data, can a third party — without consulting the author — reach the same conclusion? If not: can they identify the precise step at which they would diverge? A chain that cannot be checked is not a chain. It is a declaration.

**What this is not:** The Chain of Custody Principle does not require that the inference be simple or short. Long, complex inference chains are admissible provided every step is documented. The requirement is checkability, not brevity.

**Common failure mode:** A conclusion is supported by pointing to a large body of work that collectively implies it, without specifying which steps in which documents constitute the inference. The work may be genuine; the conclusion may be correct; but the chain is not traceable, and therefore the conclusion is not yet evidence — it is a direction to look.

---

## Interaction Between Principles

The three principles are mutually constraining:

- The Specification Principle makes the Discriminability Principle testable — without defined terms, no observation can be assessed as confirming or defeating.
- The Discriminability Principle gives the Specification Principle purpose — terms should be defined precisely enough to generate checkable predictions, not more and not less.
- The Chain of Custody Principle connects both to evidence — without a traceable chain, neither specification nor discriminability translates into scientific result.

A framework that satisfies only one or two principles is in a recognizable intermediate state. The constitutional triad makes that state visible rather than obscuring it.
