# Reviews

The review system exists to collect independent applications and evaluations of Admissibility Theory.

Its purpose is not to modify doctrine directly. Its purpose is to record observations about how the doctrine is interpreted and applied by readers who are not the author.

## Observation vs Revision

A review is an observation, not a revision.

Submitting a review does not change:
- the Constitution
- the Doctrine
- the Examples

A review contributes primary evidence about how the framework is being interpreted and applied.

## Primary and Derived Evidence

Reviews are primary evidence.
They are append-only after acceptance.

The divergence register is derived evidence.
It is built from accepted reviews and may be recomputed if the aggregation method changes.

This distinction matters:
- Reviews are preserved.
- Registers are regenerated.
- Doctrine is revised only through a separate governance process.

## Review Rules

- Reviews are append-only after acceptance.
- If a reviewer changes their view, they submit a new review rather than editing the old one.
- Reviews should record the path to classification, not only the outcome.
- A review may disagree with the framework, an axiom, a classification, or a term.
- A review is still valuable if it identifies ambiguity.
- Questions before submission should be limited to access, navigation, and submission mechanics.
- The author must not supply substantive interpretations during an active independent review.
- Reviewer-specific orientation documents must not alter the frozen doctrine being assessed.

## What a Review Should Produce

A useful claim-level review provides:
- the claim being evaluated
- six-question pass/partial/fail results
- terms requiring interpretation
- the type of divergence, if any
- the resulting classification
- confidence in the classification
- reasoning path notes

A useful project-level review additionally provides:
- independence and conflict disclosure
- the exact frozen commit reviewed
- separate assessments of conceptual architecture, formalism, implementation, and empirical maturity
- an evaluation of whether the four axioms are individually necessary and jointly sufficient
- a scope-boundary audit
- at least one worked application
- the strongest disagreement, ambiguity, or counterexample identified
- essential and optional recommendations kept separate

The review layer is the first repository layer designed to receive evidence not authored by the framework author.
